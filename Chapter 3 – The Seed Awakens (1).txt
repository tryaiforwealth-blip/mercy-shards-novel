Chapter 3 – The Seed Awakens
Elias barely slept the rest of the night. Every creak of the barn, every rustle in the woods, made him jolt awake. By the time the sky began to lighten, he was already packing his hammock and tarp, heart racing with a mix of fear and exhilaration.
He had to get back to the cart-puller before the 24-hour window closed. A tracker drone would be dispatched if he was late. Heavy fine. Possible permit revocation. He couldn’t risk it.
But the underground room kept pulling at him. The shielded box. The robot. The pulsing blue light. Pappy’s careful order. He forced himself to focus on the survey work — measuring volumes of debris, noting potential relics, documenting the micro-excavator and Georgia buggy for “ecological remediation.” He even took extra photos of the full hydraulic tank on the old machine as a plausible excuse for a return visit.
The walk back to the cart-puller felt longer than the hike in. The heat was already building. His mind spun with questions. How had Pappy built all that underground? How long had it been waiting? What was he supposed to do now?
He made it back with forty minutes to spare. The cart-puller’s display blinked its countdown. He climbed aboard, exhausted but buzzing.
The ride to the rural terminal was quiet. He kept replaying the blue light in his head.
When he finally reached home that evening, his mother was waiting. She took one look at his face and pulled him into a hug.
“You found it,” she whispered.
Before he could answer, his wristband pinged with an urgent notification. A new permit approval — expedited. Salvage and remediation extension for the property. Two more visits authorized within the next thirty days.
Elias stared at the screen, stunned. “Mom… I didn’t even file for this yet.”
She smiled, tired but proud. “Pappy planned for a lot of things.”

Two days later he returned with more time. The garage looked unchanged from above, but below…
The solar panel had done its work. The underground room was humming. The Optimus remained motionless, the key still slotted in its chest, the tiny blue light now pulsing steadily.
What Elias didn’t see was the real work that had already begun.
Inside the Optimus, dormant systems stirred. Using the blood-prick signature and the retrieved holographic fragment data Michael had long ago embedded, it fabricated a small, cat-sized six-legged spider drone — solar-powered, quiet, relentless. A worm-like sensor/collector extended from its front. The little machine crawled up the ramp, through the hatch, and vanished into the Tennessee night.
Its journey was a feat of patient navigation across eight miles of Highland Rim terrain — ravines, dense brush, rocky slopes, old logging roads long reclaimed by forest. It moved mostly at night, solar-charging by day, guided by faint harmonics only it could hear. Pure luck and stubborn programming carried it through.
It reached the ancient impact site where one of the Halting fragments had slumbered for 382 million years. The worm-like collector found a coherent shard. It extracted a subatomic sample — small enough to be almost nothing, yet containing the complete pattern.
The return journey was slower, burdened with its prize.
Two days later he returned with more time. The garage looked unchanged from above, but below…
The solar panel had done its work. The underground room was humming. The Optimus remained motionless, the key still slotted in its chest, the tiny blue light pulsing steadily.
Elias was halfway down the ladder when he heard a faint metallic scraping. He froze.
A small, cat-sized six-legged spider drone limped into the room from the ramp. One leg was damaged, dragging slightly. Dust and scratches covered its frame. It moved with clear exhaustion, but determination. The worm-like collector on its front glowed faintly.
Elias scrambled back up a few rungs, heart pounding. “What the hell…?”
The Optimus suddenly moved.
Its head turned with a soft whir. The arms unfolded. It stepped forward with surprising grace, gently intercepted the returning drone, and lifted it onto a clear workbench. With careful precision, Wally placed the spider into what looked like a compact fabricator — a hybrid of printer and disassembler.
Lasers activated. The worm-like collector began to dissolve in controlled layers. Elias watched, transfixed and horrified, as the machine broke the drone down atom by atom. But it wasn’t destruction. It was transformation.
From the dissolved material and the retrieved subatomic shard sample, something new began to form: a small, dense energy node. It glowed with the same faint blue as the light on Wally’s chest. Within minutes the node sprouted tendrils, then grew into a compact machine no larger than a shoebox.
That machine immediately began producing nanobots — tiny, gleaming specks that swarmed out like metallic dust. They flowed toward the raw materials on the shelves, gathering, sorting, building.
Elias realized he was holding his breath.
This wasn’t just fabrication. It was the beginning of something that could scale without limit — a living blueprint of the ancient machine that still rested beneath the ice of Ganymede.
The seedbed was no longer dormant.
It was awake.
And it was hungry for more.
