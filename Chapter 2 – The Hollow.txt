Chapter 2 – The Hollow
Dawn broke with a start. A raccoon had gotten into his pack and was rifling through a ration pouch. The creature looked at him with bold, unafraid eyes before sauntering off behind some vine-covered boards leaning against the barn’s broken wall.
Elias’s heart hammered. “Damn thing scared the shit out of me.”
The raccoon’s escape dislodged debris. In the beam of his flashlight, something glinted on the concrete floor — a small metal hatch, half-hidden under years of dust and fallen roofing.
He approached carefully, brushing away dirt and leaves. The hatch was rusted but solid, with a keyhole in the center. His pulse quickened. The old brass key his mother had given him felt heavy in his pocket.
It’s a grave.
The thought hit him like cold water. The scanner confirmed an enclosed cavity beneath the garage. Protocol would demand he radio it in immediately. If there were human remains, the site would be locked down. His family would lose any chance to learn what really happened to Pappy. So he didn’t radio.
Just one look. Just enough to know.
He slid the brass key into the lock. It turned with a gritty click. Cool air rose as he lifted the hatch. A metal ladder disappeared into darkness. No smell of decay. Just silence.
He climbed down.
At the bottom, a heavy out-swing door blocked further progress. A small lockbox hung from it. The childhood rhyme his mother had taught him gave the code. Inside the box lay a strange, dense key. As he turned it over in his hand, a tiny prick stung his finger — blood and something else, a faint metallic taste in the air.
The key slid into the door’s mechanism. He pried it open with a crowbar he’d spotted in the garage above. The door released with a loud hiss — preserved air rushing out of a space that had been sealed for decades.
The underground room was a rectangle, roughly twenty by thirty feet. A gentle ramp led up to what looked like hydraulic hatch doors at the far end — an emergency exit or loading ramp, long sealed. The walls were lined with heavy foil. The air was cool and dry.
It was a survivor’s laboratory.
Shelves lined the walls, packed with supplies: solar panels still in wrapping, banks of old but serviceable batteries, high-end GPUs, tools, raw materials, printed manuals, and early technical documents. Some of the surveyor equipment looked suspiciously high-end for a private citizen. A few items might even be illegal under Core regulations.
In the center of the room stood a coffin-like shielded box, heavy and matte black, clearly designed to survive an EMP that never came.
No bones. No body.
Relief crashed over him, followed immediately by a deeper unease. If Pappy didn’t die here… where did he go?
Elias carried a small solar panel on its tripod outside and set it up facing the anticipated sunrise. It would still be hours before the sun cleared the treeline, but every bit helped. He forced himself to do legitimate survey work — measuring volumes of debris, noting the old micro-excavator — as cover for the 24-hour clock ticking above him.
He went back down once the panel had charged for about an hour. A row of lights flickered on. Something started to hum faintly in the walls.
He approached the shielded box, undid the latches, and looked inside.
Resting on a padded cradle was an early residential Optimus Gen-1 — squat, boxy, its binocular eyes staring blankly upward. A thin layer of dust covered it, but the solar charger on its shoulder was already glowing.
On the chest plate was a small slot. The strange key — still warm from his hand — fit perfectly.
A faint, tiny blue light began flashing on the robot’s frame. Slow. Patient. Like a heartbeat.
Elias stared at it, haunted and fascinated. He should remove the key. He should close everything up and get back to the cart-puller. Instead, in his haste and exhaustion, he left it there. Solar collection active. Blue light flashing in the cool darkness.
He climbed out, sealed the hatch as best he could, and made it back to the cart-puller with minutes to spare.
All the way home, that tiny flashing light followed him.
