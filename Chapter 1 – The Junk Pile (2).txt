Chapter 1 – The Junk Pile
Elias Sparks pressed his forehead against the cool composite glass of the aero-bus window, watching the endless grid of managed farmland and vertical growing towers slide beneath them. The old interstate appeared in broken patches, concrete overtaken by vines and scrub. The vehicle itself was a squat, humming rectangle in dull regulatory gray, every surface stamped with the Core’s interlocking-circle logo. It smelled of recycled air and someone else’s lunch. He was the last passenger.
He was in his late twenties, restless, and trying hard not to show it.
On his personal device, an old archival documentary played low: Elon Musk: The Last Dreamer. Grainy 2020s footage showed a wild-haired man talking about Starship, Mars, and making humanity multi-planetary because life was too precious to keep all its eggs in one fragile basket.
Elias knew the lines by heart. He also knew the narration that always followed — how the dream had been too fast, too threatening to the careful order being built. Oversight boards. Global safety accords. Monopoly hearings. The explosive momentum had been gently leashed, redirected, and finally absorbed into the Core’s managed stability programs. The stars were now for logistics, not dreamers.
He killed the feed.
The aero-bus banked, descending toward the rural terminal. Below, the wilderness looked almost pristine — part of the Global Wildlife Protection Corridor. Most people lived in the pods now. Clean. Safe. Sufficient. Your Value Index determined your apartment, your stipend, your entertainment credits, even the square meters you were entitled to fill with possessions. The Core had solved scarcity so completely that wanting more than your share felt… antisocial.
His mother had fought for months to get him this assignment. A salvage permit on an old family property in Middle Tennessee — his great-grandfather’s barn and acreage. Officially, it was ecological remediation. Unofficially, she had steered his surveyor certification for years so he would be the one qualified to go.
“Pappy left something for whoever would listen,” she’d told him the night before, pressing the old brass key into his hand. “But you’ll have to look for it.”
The bus settled. Elias stepped into the humid late-June heat, pack slung over one shoulder. The single autonomous cart-puller waited — low, treaded, deliberately stupid. No advanced AI. Perfect for restricted rural zones.
He climbed aboard. The display confirmed his 24-hour permit. Miss the return window and a tracker drone would be dispatched. Heavy fine. Possible restriction upgrade.
As the puller lurched onto the cracked access road, Elias started talking to the recorder clipped to his collar, just like she’d asked.
“Log 01.
I don’t know what I’m supposed to find out here. But Mom says Pappy believed the spoken word still matters. So… I guess I’m talking.”
He talked about the documentary. About how the world had slowly traded risk for comfort, wonder for sufficiency. About how most people now lived in the pods, their lives optimized, their possessions limited to what their Value Index entitled them to. Even the Surveyor Corps — his own job — only existed because human eyes and hands were still better at reading context, aesthetics, and the quiet stories old things carried. A rusty Sony Walkman might hold enough minerals for three modern medical sensors, but the Core still wanted a human to log its history before it was recycled.
The cart-puller hummed deeper into rewilded land. Tall grass and young saplings pressed close. Elias kept talking.
The cart-puller stopped at the old Allens Market intersection. Elias slung his pack and began the final three-mile walk.
The first mile and a half were relatively easy, following faint old paths through recovering forest. Then the trail dropped into a steep gully. He picked his way down and crossed the Blackburn Fork river just above the falls, the mist cool on his face and the sound of water rushing below. From there the path climbed a darkly forested hill. At the top he took a right turn past the first cut in the ridge, the watershed opening in the direction of the old Flynn Creek area beyond.
The garage was worse than he expected — vinyl siding peeling, roof partially collapsed, piles of junk and fallen roofing making the interior too unstable to enter safely. He set up camp on the old house foundation instead: hammock, tarp, mosquito net.
As dusk settled, the sounds of the recovering woods surrounded him. Elias sat on the edge of the foundation, staring at the sagging structure.
Somewhere in there — or beneath it — might be what was left of Pappy.
He didn’t say the part that sat heaviest in his chest.
Every collapsed roof and shallow depression ahead might hold what was left of Pappy.
And for the first time in a long time, Elias Sparks felt like he might actually be stepping off the approved path.
